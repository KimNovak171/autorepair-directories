The term "tune-up" has been part of automotive vocabulary for generations, but what it means has changed dramatically. On cars from the 1960s and 70s, a tune-up involved adjusting points, setting the timing, and cleaning or replacing a distributor cap — all part of the mechanical ignition system. Modern vehicles have no points, no distributor, and no timing adjustments. So what does a tune-up actually mean today?

## The Modern Tune-Up

Today's tune-up centers on the ignition system — specifically the spark plugs — along with a few other related components and an inspection of systems that affect engine performance. Here's what's typically included:

### Spark Plug Replacement

Spark plugs ignite the compressed air-fuel mixture in each cylinder. Over time, the electrode wears, the gap widens, and the spark becomes weaker or less consistent. Misfires — incomplete combustion in one or more cylinders — result in rough running, power loss, poor fuel economy, and elevated hydrocarbon emissions.

Modern iridium or platinum-tipped spark plugs last significantly longer than the old copper-tip plugs. Many manufacturers specify 60,000 to 100,000-mile intervals. But "lasts longer" doesn't mean infinite — eventually even premium plugs degrade. Replacement is the core of any modern tune-up.

### Air Filter Replacement

The engine air filter prevents dust, dirt, and debris from entering the engine through the intake. A clogged air filter restricts airflow, causing the engine to run rich (too much fuel relative to air), which reduces power and fuel efficiency. Air filters are inexpensive and typically replaced every 15,000 to 30,000 miles depending on environment (dusty conditions require more frequent changes).

### Fuel Filter Replacement (Where Applicable)

Fuel filters remove contaminants from the fuel supply before it reaches the injectors. On older vehicles, these were external and straightforward to replace. On many modern vehicles, the fuel filter is integrated into the fuel pump assembly inside the tank, which makes it a much more involved replacement. Check your owner's manual — some manufacturers specify replacement at 30,000 to 60,000 miles, others don't list a service interval for in-tank filters.

### PCV Valve Inspection/Replacement

The Positive Crankcase Ventilation (PCV) valve routes combustion gases from the crankcase back into the intake to be burned rather than vented to atmosphere. A stuck or clogged PCV valve causes pressure buildup in the crankcase, which can push oil past seals and gaskets. It's a small, inexpensive part that's often included in tune-up services.

### Ignition Wires or Coil-On-Plug Boots (Where Applicable)

Vehicles with a single coil and distributor use ignition wires to route high voltage to each spark plug. These wires degrade over time and can develop cracks or internal breaks that cause misfires. Most modern vehicles use coil-on-plug (COP) systems with no wires — each plug has its own coil mounted directly on top. The boots on COP systems can degrade but are generally less of a routine concern than traditional wires.

## What a Tune-Up Is NOT

A tune-up is not a catch-all term for general maintenance. It's specifically about combustion and ignition performance. It doesn't replace your brakes, change your oil, service your transmission, or address your cooling system. If a shop quotes you a "tune-up" and includes dozens of items not related to ignition and combustion, they may be bundling unrelated services under a familiar name to increase the bill.

## When Do You Need One?

Signs that suggest tune-up components need attention:

- Rough idling or engine stumbling
- Noticeable loss of acceleration or power
- Engine misfires (often felt as a rhythmic jerk or shudder)
- Decreased fuel economy that can't be explained by driving conditions
- Check engine light with misfire codes (P0300 through P0308)
- The vehicle hasn't had spark plugs replaced within the manufacturer's recommended interval

Even without symptoms, following the manufacturer's spark plug replacement interval is good preventive maintenance. Worn plugs don't always produce obvious symptoms until they're quite far gone.

## Cost of a Modern Tune-Up

Cost varies significantly based on the vehicle and the number of cylinders. More cylinders mean more spark plugs. On an accessible four-cylinder engine, a tune-up might cost $150 to $250. On a V8 engine with difficult plug access, it can run $400 or more. Luxury vehicles with turbocharged engines often require more labor.

Ask for an itemized quote and understand exactly what's being replaced before you authorize the work. A proper tune-up is worth the investment — better combustion means better performance, fuel economy, and reduced emissions.
## Keeping Records of Tune-Up Services

Every tune-up service should be documented — ideally with a shop receipt showing the parts installed, part numbers, date, and mileage. This documentation is valuable for two reasons: it helps you track when the next interval is due without relying on memory, and it provides evidence of maintenance history if you sell the vehicle. A buyer who sees documented spark plug replacement at 95,000 miles on a 120,000-mile vehicle is far more confident in the engine's condition than one receiving only a verbal assurance. Keep receipts in the glove box or in a dedicated vehicle folder at home. Your investment in the tune-up is also an investment in the vehicle's documented value.
