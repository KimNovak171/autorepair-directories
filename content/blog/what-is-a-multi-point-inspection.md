A multi-point inspection (MPI) is a systematic review of your vehicle's key systems and components, performed by a technician while the car is already in the shop — usually during an oil change or other routine service. It's one of the most useful tools available to car owners who want to stay ahead of maintenance needs and catch developing problems before they become failures.

## What a Multi-Point Inspection Covers

While the specific checklist varies by shop, a thorough multi-point inspection typically includes evaluation of:

**Tires:** Tread depth measured at multiple points (inside, center, and outside of each tire), pressure, sidewall condition, and visible damage.

**Brakes:** Pad thickness (measured in millimeters or fractions of an inch), rotor condition (thickness, scoring, or warping), caliper condition, and hardware.

**Fluids:** Engine oil level and condition, coolant level, brake fluid level, power steering fluid (where applicable), windshield washer fluid, and transmission fluid (if accessible).

**Belts and hoses:** Serpentine belt condition (cracking, fraying, glazing), radiator and heater hoses (cracks, sponginess, swelling), and coolant hose connections.

**Battery and charging system:** Battery voltage and load test, terminal condition, alternator output.

**Lights:** All exterior lights — headlights (high and low beam), brake lights, turn signals, reverse lights, hazard lights, and license plate lights.

**Wipers:** Blade condition and effectiveness.

**Filters:** Engine air filter and cabin air filter condition.

**Steering and suspension:** Visible inspection of tie rod ends, ball joints, control arm bushings, shocks/struts, and sway bar links for play, wear, or damage.

**Exhaust system:** Visible inspection of the exhaust for leaks, damage, or excessive rust.

Some shops also check the TPMS sensors, cabin air quality, fuel cap seal, and other items depending on their protocol.

## How Results Are Typically Presented

Most shops present MPI results using a color-coded system:

**Green:** Item is in good condition — no action needed at this time.

**Yellow/Amber:** Item shows wear or is approaching service interval — monitor and plan for service in the near future.

**Red:** Item is unsafe or critically worn — immediate attention required.

This color coding gives you a quick visual summary of where your vehicle stands across dozens of components, letting you prioritize concerns and plan maintenance accordingly.

## How to Use the Information

The MPI is a starting point, not a final repair authorization. When a shop presents you with inspection results, you have several options:

**For green items:** No action needed. File the report as a reference point for future comparisons.

**For yellow items:** Understand the timeframe. A technician should be able to tell you whether "watch this" means address it in the next 1,000 miles or the next 10,000. Get a quote and plan accordingly.

**For red items:** Take the urgency seriously, but also verify. For a major repair recommendation following an inspection you didn't initially request, it's reasonable to get a second opinion — especially if the cost is significant. A reputable shop will understand this.

## What Makes an MPI Trustworthy

The quality and honesty of an MPI depends entirely on the shop performing it. Signs of a trustworthy inspection:

- **Measurements, not just opinions.** Brake pad thickness should be a specific number ("3mm remaining"), not just "getting low." Tire tread depth should be measured, not eyeballed. Shops with the right tools and habits provide data.

- **Consistent results across visits.** If you get an MPI at the same shop twice in six months and the results are wildly different with no explanation, ask questions.

- **No undue urgency.** A trustworthy shop presents findings and lets you make decisions. High-pressure insistence that a yellow item requires immediate repair is a warning sign.

- **Willingness to show you.** A good shop will put your vehicle on the lift and show you a worn brake pad, a cracked belt, or a leaking hose if you want to see it. Transparency is a hallmark of quality service.

## A Free Service Worth Taking Seriously

Most shops include a multi-point inspection at no extra charge with oil changes or other services. Despite being free, many drivers don't review the results carefully, if they read them at all. Treating the inspection as a genuine health report for your vehicle — reading it, asking questions, and using it to plan your next 12 months of maintenance — is one of the most effective ways to stay on top of vehicle care without being reactive.

Keep the report. Compare it to the next one. Watch for items that move from green to yellow over time — those are your upcoming maintenance priorities.
## Decline, Monitor, or Act: Your Options Are Yours

One thing worth emphasizing: the MPI is advisory, not mandatory. When a shop presents findings, they're informing you — not billing you automatically. You're always in control of what work you authorize. If a yellow item comes up and you want to monitor it for another 3,000 miles before deciding, that's your call. If a red item is flagged and you want a second opinion before spending $800, that's completely reasonable. A shop that respects you will present the findings clearly and then wait for your decision without pressure. Use the information to make smart, informed choices — not to feel rushed into authorizing work you're not ready for.
